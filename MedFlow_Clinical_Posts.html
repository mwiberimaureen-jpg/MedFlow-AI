MedFlow AI

Clinical Reasoning Posts — 2-Week Series

Format: Clinical clue → MedFlow differentials + tests → Impression  |  X & TikTok

How to Use These Posts

On X: Post the hook block as the opening tweet. Post the MedFlow output + impression as the first reply (thread). This keeps the hook clean and the clinical detail accessible for those who want it.

On TikTok: Read the hook aloud (0–15s). Pause after the clue line. Then: "Here's what MedFlow gave for this case" — screen-record the MedFlow output being generated (15–55s). Close with the impression (55–60s).

  • Never read the differentials robotically — say "MedFlow flagged three things" and walk through them conversationally.
  • Pause after the clue line every time. That pause is where the viewer stops scrolling.
  • The impression line is the payoff. Deliver it slowly and with confidence.

Week 1

Internal medicine, respiratory, cardiology, endocrinology, neurology

1
Monday Respiratory / Infectious
Hook
You treated "community-acquired pneumonia" with amoxicillin for 5 days.No improvement. Temperature still 38.9. Night sweats started.It was never pneumonia. The chest X-ray just looked that way.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Pulmonary Tuberculosis
Sputum AFB x3, GeneXpert MTB/RIF, Mantoux/IGRA, CXR (upper lobe pattern, cavitation).
Rules in: positive AFB smear or GeneXpert. Rules out: negative GeneXpert + negative culture at 6 weeks (with low pre-test probability).
Differential 2 — Pulmonary Lymphoma
CT chest (hilar/mediastinal adenopathy), LDH, serum protein electrophoresis, bronchoscopy with BAL and biopsy.
Rules in: hilar adenopathy + histological confirmation on biopsy. Rules out: normal CT and LDH with alternative diagnosis confirmed.
Differential 3 — Pulmonary Vasculitis (GPA)
ANCA (PR3/MPO), urine dipstick for haematuria, CT chest (cavitating nodules), renal function.
Rules in: positive PR3-ANCA + cavitating lesions + renal involvement + biopsy. Rules out: negative ANCA with alternative diagnosis confirmed.
MedFlow Impression
Pulmonary Tuberculosis
Antibiotic failure at 5 days + night sweats + upper lobe pattern = TB until proven otherwise. Isolate the patient. Initiate sputum collection. Refer to infectious disease before changing antibiotics empirically.

MedFlow Impression
Coeliac Disease
Non-response to oral iron in a young woman is malabsorption until proven otherwise. Screen with anti-tTG IgA before scoping. Do not increase the iron dose — find why it is not working.

Wednesday Respiratory / ENT
Hook
Patient admitted with a "flare of asthma." Salbutamol given. No relief.You listen again. The wheeze is loudest over the trachea, not the lung bases.It was never asthma. The obstruction is above the carina.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Tracheal Stenosis
CT neck and chest (axial cuts through trachea), flow-volume loop (inspiratory plateau pattern), direct laryngoscopy.
Rules in: fixed narrowing on CT + flat inspiratory loop. Rules out: normal CT tracheal diameter + normal flow-volume loop.
Differential 2 — Laryngeal or Tracheal Mass
Nasolaryngoscopy, CT neck with contrast, biopsy of any identified lesion.
Rules in: mass lesion on scope with histological confirmation. Rules out: normal laryngoscopy and CT.
Differential 3 — Vocal Cord Dysfunction
Flow-volume loop (variable extrathoracic obstruction — flattened inspiratory limb), laryngoscopy during symptomatic episode.
Rules in: paradoxical vocal cord adduction on laryngoscopy during episode. Rules out: normal laryngoscopy during symptoms.
MedFlow Impression
Upper Airway Obstruction
Monophonic wheeze loudest over the trachea is a large airway sign, not a small airway sign. Salbutamol will not help. Stop it. Get ENT. The flow-volume loop is the next investigation — not another nebuliser.

Thursday Cardiology / Rheumatology
Hook
28-year-old. Hypertension found incidentally. Started on amlodipine.You check BP in both arms. Right: 160/100. Left: 112/74.The asymmetry just told you this isn't essential hypertension.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Takayasu's Arteritis
ESR, CRP, CT angiography of aorta and branches (vessel wall thickening, stenosis, aneurysm), PET-CT if CT equivocal.
Rules in: vessel wall inflammation/stenosis on CTA + elevated inflammatory markers in a young woman. Rules out: normal CTA with alternative vascular cause identified.
Differential 2 — Coarctation of the Aorta (proximal to left subclavian)
4-limb BP measurement (arm > leg), ECG (LVH), CXR (rib notching, figure-of-3 sign), CT aortogram, echocardiogram.
Rules in: pressure gradient on imaging + arm-leg BP discrepancy. Rules out: normal CT aortogram.
Differential 3 — Aortic Dissection (Stanford B, involving left subclavian)
Urgent CT angiography chest/abdomen/pelvis.
Rules in: intimal flap on CTA. Rules out: no dissection on CTA.
MedFlow Impression
Takayasu's Arteritis
Young patient + inter-arm BP difference >10mmHg + hypertension = large vessel vasculitis until excluded. Do not simply titrate the antihypertensive. Get the CTA. Check ESR and CRP today.

Friday Neurology / Nephrology
Hook
Patient came in confused. CT head normal. Lumbar puncture normal.You review the drug chart. She's been on 80mg furosemide daily for two weeks.The brain is fine. Check the sodium.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Diuretic-Induced Hyponatraemia
Serum Na, serum osmolality, urine Na, urine osmolality, serum K (co-depletion common).
Rules in: serum Na <130 + urine Na >20 on loop diuretic + resolution with electrolyte correction. Rules out: normal serum Na.
Differential 2 — Hypomagnesaemia
Serum magnesium (often co-depleted with loop diuretics and frequently not checked).
Rules in: Mg <0.7mmol/L + symptom resolution on replacement. Rules out: normal serum magnesium.
Differential 3 — Wernicke's Encephalopathy
Thiamine level, MRI brain (mamillary body and periaqueductal signal change), empirical IV thiamine 500mg TDS x3 days if clinical suspicion high.
Rules in: clinical triad (confusion + ophthalmoplegia + ataxia) + MRI findings. Rules out: no nutritional risk factors + alternative diagnosis confirmed.
MedFlow Impression
Diuretic-Induced Hyponatraemia
Loop diuretic + confusion + normal structural imaging = electrolyte cause first. Draw UECs before ordering the next investigation. Hyponatraemia at this level is enough to explain a GCS of 14.

Saturday Cardiology / Nephrology
Hook
You gave 2 litres of IV saline to a patient in "dehydration."An hour later she's more short of breath than when she arrived.You treated the wrong diagnosis. Now find the right one.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Acute Decompensated Heart Failure
BNP or NT-proBNP, CXR (pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines), ECG, echocardiogram.
Rules in: elevated BNP + pulmonary oedema on CXR + echo showing reduced EF or diastolic dysfunction. Rules out: normal BNP + normal CXR + normal echo.
Differential 2 — SIADH Worsened by Isotonic Saline
Serum Na, serum osmolality, urine Na, urine osmolality (urine osmolality >100mOsm/kg in a hypotonic patient = SIADH).
Rules in: euvolaemic hyponatraemia + urine Na >40 + urine osmolality > serum osmolality. Rules out: normal serum Na.
Differential 3 — Nephrotic Syndrome
Urine protein:creatinine ratio, serum albumin, lipid profile, renal USS, renal biopsy if indicated.
Rules in: proteinuria >3.5g/day + hypoalbuminaemia <30 + oedema. Rules out: normal urine protein and albumin.
MedFlow Impression
Acute Decompensated Cardiac Failure
Fluid-responsive dehydration improves with fluid. If the patient deteriorates after 2L of saline, the diagnosis was wrong. Check the BNP. Get the CXR. The fluid may have just tipped her into pulmonary oedema.

Sunday Endocrinology
Hook
19-year-old male. Brought in by his mother. "He's been sad for months."On examination: slow movements, dry skin, cold hands, constipation, bradycardia.This isn't depression. Check the TSH.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Primary Hypothyroidism
TSH (sensitive first-line test), free T4. If TSH elevated, confirm with free T4 before treating.
Rules in: elevated TSH + low free T4. Rules out: normal TSH (primary hypothyroidism excluded; consider central if high suspicion).
Differential 2 — Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)
Morning cortisol (8am), ACTH stimulation test, serum electrolytes (hyponatraemia + hyperkalaemia), ACTH level.
Rules in: failure to mount cortisol response >550nmol/L on ACTH stimulation + electrolyte pattern. Rules out: normal cortisol response.
Differential 3 — Young-Onset Parkinson's Disease
Clinical assessment (resting tremor, cogwheel rigidity, postural instability), DAT-SPECT scan, neurology referral.
Rules in: asymmetric parkinsonism + dopamine transporter deficit on SPECT. Rules out: no extrapyramidal features + alternative diagnosis confirmed.
MedFlow Impression
Primary Hypothyroidism
Bradycardia + cold intolerance + constipation + psychomotor slowing in a 19-year-old is a metabolic diagnosis first. Check the TSH before starting an antidepressant. It costs almost nothing and takes 24 hours.

8
Monday Gastroenterology
Hook
Epigastric pain. Worse at night. Relieved by eating. Treated as gastritis for 3 months.You scope him. Ulcers in the stomach and the third part of the duodenum simultaneously.One ulcer is a problem. Multiple ulcers in unusual locations is a diagnosis.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (Gastrinoma)
Fasting serum gastrin (off PPI x2 weeks), secretin stimulation test (gold standard), CT/MRI pancreas and duodenum, somatostatin receptor scintigraphy (Octreoscan).
Rules in: fasting gastrin >1000pg/mL + >200pg/mL rise after secretin + pancreatic/duodenal mass. Rules out: normal fasting gastrin on adequate PPI washout.
Differential 2 — H. pylori with Complicated Ulcer Disease
Rapid urease test on biopsy (at endoscopy), urea breath test (off PPI x2 weeks), stool antigen.
Rules in: positive H. pylori + typical single/duodenal ulcer pattern + response to eradication. Rules out: negative testing on adequate washout.
Differential 3 — Crohn's Disease (Gastroduodenal)
CT enterography, ileocolonoscopy with biopsies, faecal calprotectin, CRP.
Rules in: transmural granulomatous inflammation on biopsy + skip lesions on CT. Rules out: normal ileocolonoscopy and CT enterography.
MedFlow Impression
Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
Distal duodenal ulcer + multiple simultaneous ulcers + PPI failure = gastrinoma until excluded. Do not scope and discharge. Check the fasting gastrin. If elevated, the tumour needs to be found — it may be malignant.

Tuesday Surgery / Post-operative
Hook
Post-op day 2. Patient spiked a fever of 38.4°C.You start antibiotics.The fever on post-op day 2 is almost never infection. You just treated the wrong thing.
Here's what MedFlow offered — The 5 W's of Post-operative Fever
Wind — Days 1–2 (Atelectasis)
Clinical: decreased air entry, dullness at bases. CXR. Incentive spirometry, chest physiotherapy, early mobilisation.
Rules in: clinical + CXR findings. Treatment: physiotherapy, NOT antibiotics.
Water — Days 3–5 (UTI)
Urine dipstick, urine MC&S. Common if urinary catheter in situ.
Rules in: positive nitrites/leucocytes on dipstick + positive MC&S. Treat per sensitivities.
Wound — Days 5–7 (Surgical Site Infection)
Wound inspection (erythema, induration, discharge), wound swab MC&S.
Rules in: clinical signs of wound infection + positive swab culture.
Walking — Days 5+ (DVT / PE)
D-dimer, USS doppler lower limbs, CTPA if PE suspected. Wells score to guide investigation.
Rules in: positive CTPA or USS. Anticoagulate immediately if confirmed.
Wonder Drugs — Any Day (Drug Fever)
Review drug chart. Withdrawal of offending agent. Diagnosis of exclusion.
Rules in: fever resolves within 72 hours of drug withdrawal. Rules out: fever persists after withdrawal.
MedFlow Impression
Post-operative Atelectasis (Day 2)
Day 2 post-op fever is atelectasis until proven otherwise. Encourage deep breathing and early ambulation. Hold antibiotics unless a specific infectious source is identified. Starting antibiotics reflexively on day 2 fever is one of the most common errors on surgical wards.

Wednesday Haematology / Rheumatology
Hook
32-year-old woman. Second unprovoked DVT in 18 months. No surgery, no long flights.You ask about family history. Her mother had a stroke at 40.Unprovoked. Recurrent. Familial. The clot is a symptom, not the diagnosis.

Lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin IgG/IgM, anti-β2-glycoprotein I IgG/IgM — must be positive on two occasions at least 12 weeks apart.

MedFlow Impression
Antiphospholipid Syndrome
Young woman + recurrent unprovoked venous thrombosis + family history of early arterial event = APS first. Test before labelling her a thrombophilia and committing to lifelong anticoagulation without a diagnosis. The antibody panel is the starting point.

Thursday Haematology
Hook
Patient's urine turned dark brown after an acute illness.LFTs: normal. Bilirubin: normal. Urobilinogen: elevated.The liver is not the problem. Something is happening upstream of it.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Haemolytic Anaemia
FBC + blood film (spherocytes, schistocytes, polychromasia), reticulocyte count, LDH, haptoglobin (low in haemolysis), direct Coombs test (DAT), bilirubin fractions (unconjugated elevated).
Rules in: raised LDH + low haptoglobin + raised unconjugated bilirubin + abnormal blood film. Rules out: normal haptoglobin + normal blood film.
Differential 2 — Rhabdomyolysis
Serum CK (markedly elevated), urine myoglobin, UECs (acute kidney injury — the main complication), urine dipstick (blood positive, no RBCs on microscopy).
Rules in: CK >1000 IU/L + myoglobinuria + urine blood positive without RBCs on microscopy. Rules out: normal CK.
Differential 3 — G6PD Deficiency (Oxidative Haemolysis)
G6PD enzyme assay (test after acute episode resolves — reticulocytes can give falsely normal result acutely), blood film (bite cells, Heinz bodies on supravital stain).
Rules in: enzyme deficiency on assay + bite cells on film + trigger identified (acute illness, drug, food). Rules out: normal enzyme level on repeat testing when stable.
MedFlow Impression
Haemolytic Anaemia — Query G6PD Deficiency
Dark urine after acute illness with normal LFTs = red cell destruction, not liver disease. Elevated urobilinogen confirms the bilirubin load is coming from haemolysis. Check the film. Check the enzyme — but wait until the acute episode settles for an accurate result.

Friday Obstetrics
Hook
Pregnant woman at 32 weeks. BP 148/96. Mild headache.Urine: 2+ protein. Platelets: 89. LFTs elevated.She doesn't have hypertension. She has a syndrome — and it's worsening.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — HELLP Syndrome
FBC (thrombocytopaenia, anaemia), blood film (schistocytes — microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia), LDH (elevated), LFTs (AST/ALT elevated), uric acid, urine protein:creatinine ratio.

Blood film (schistocytes), ADAMTS13 activity level, LDH, renal function.

MedFlow Impression
HELLP Syndrome
Thrombocytopaenia + elevated LFTs + proteinuric hypertension at 32 weeks = HELLP. This is not managed outpatient. Admit immediately. Obstetrics and haematology today. Delivery timing is a clinical decision — but the decision needs to happen now.

Saturday Neurology / Geriatrics
Hook
Elderly man on warfarin. Collapsed at home. GCS 14 on arrival. CT head: normal.The fall was 3 weeks ago.A normal CT today doesn't mean what you think it means.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation

MRI brain (isodense haematoma invisible on standard CT at 3 weeks — MRI FLAIR/T1 identifies it), repeat CT at 4–6 weeks if MRI unavailable, neurosurgery review.

MedFlow Impression
Chronic Subdural Haematoma
Warfarin + head trauma 3 weeks ago + fluctuating GCS = isodense subdural on CT that looks completely normal. CT sensitivity at 3 weeks is approximately 70%. A normal CT is not a normal brain. Get the MRI. Reverse the anticoagulation while you wait for it.

Monday Paediatrics / Neurology
Hook
You gave the anticonvulsant. The seizure stopped.You wrote "febrile convulsion" and planned discharge.Her capillary blood glucose was 1.2 mmol/L. Nobody had checked.She was back 4 hours later — seizing again. The fever was never the cause.
Convulsions in Children — Classification of Causes
Metabolic Causes — Remember MATCH
M — Magnesium low (hypomagnesaemia — neonates, malnutrition) A — Ammonia high (inborn errors of metabolism, urea cycle defects) T — Timber! Glucose low (hypoglycaemia — the most common missed metabolic cause; check BGL in every seizing child) C — Calcium low (hypocalcaemia — neonates, vitamin D deficiency, hypoparathyroidism) H — Hyponatraemia (Na <125 mmol/L — dilutional, gastroenteritis with hypotonic fluids) First-line bedside tests: capillary blood glucose, Na, Ca, Mg.
Rule: "Febrile convulsion" is a diagnosis of exclusion. Check the glucose before you use that label — every time, without exception.
Infectious Causes — Remember BEAM
B — Bacterial meningitis (fever + seizure + neck stiffness or bulging fontanelle → LP after stabilisation; do NOT delay antibiotics for imaging in a sick child) E — Encephalitis (viral — HSV most important; altered consciousness + personality change + fever → aciclovir empirically while awaiting PCR) A — Abscess (cerebral — subacute onset, focal neurological signs, raised ICP → CT with contrast first) M — Malaria (cerebral malaria — P. falciparum; any febrile child with seizures in an endemic region → thick & thin blood film immediately) Key investigations: LP (if safe), blood cultures, thick film, HSV PCR on CSF.
Rule: In a child under 18 months, absence of neck stiffness does NOT exclude bacterial meningitis. LP is mandatory if no contraindications.
Structural / Epileptic Causes
Epilepsy (first unprovoked afebrile seizure → EEG + MRI brain) Neurocysticercosis (ring-enhancing lesions on CT — endemic area, exposure to undercooked pork; serology + CT head) Space-occupying lesion / vascular malformation (focal onset, focal signs → urgent CT) Hypoxic-ischaemic injury (perinatal history — relevant in neonates) CT head indications: focal signs, prolonged seizure (>30 min), first unprovoked seizure, no metabolic or infectious cause identified.
Rule: A normal CT does not rule out epilepsy — EEG is the key investigation for epilepsy, not imaging.
MedFlow Impression
Every Seizing Child Gets a BGL and Na — Before Any Other Label
MATCH the metabolic causes. BEAM through the infectious ones. "Febrile convulsion" only applies to a child over 6 months with a simple, brief, generalised seizure who has no signs of CNS infection and a normal BGL and electrolytes. Before you write it — prove it. One missed glucose is one preventable readmission.

Tuesday Paediatrics / Emergency
Hook
18-month-old. 3 days of watery diarrhoea. Sunken eyes. Crying without tears.Skin pinch stays up for 2 seconds. Pulse rapid and weak. Lethargic.You know he needs fluids. But which plan — and at exactly what rate?Most trainees give the right fluid. They give it at the wrong speed.
Dehydration in Paediatrics — WHO/IMCI Plan A, B, C
Step 1 — Assess Severity: The ESS Signs (Eyes, Skin, State)
No dehydration: Alert, normal eyes, normal skin turgor, drinks normally → Plan A Some dehydration (5–10%): Restless/irritable, sunken eyes, skin pinch returns slowly (1–2 sec), drinks eagerly → Plan B Severe dehydration (>10%): Lethargic or unconscious, very sunken eyes, skin pinch stays up >2 seconds, unable to drink, weak or absent pulse → Plan C One of these three signs = severe: skin stays up, lethargic, unable to drink.
Mnemonic — ESS: Eyes · Skin · State. If one of the "severe" ESS signs is present, go straight to Plan C. Do not use the oral route in severe dehydration.
Plan A, B, C — The ORS Ladder
Plan A (No dehydration — prevention at home): → ORS 10 ml/kg after each loose stool. Continue breastfeeding and normal feeds. Zinc 20 mg/day for 10 days (<6 months: 10 mg/day). Plan B (Some dehydration — ORS in facility): → ORS 75 ml/kg over 4 hours, given in small sips. Reassess every 1–2 hours. Improving → continue. Worsening → escalate to Plan C. Plan C (Severe dehydration — IV fluids): → IV Ringer's Lactate (preferred) or Normal Saline → Age ≤12 months: 30 ml/kg over 1 hour, then 70 ml/kg over 5 hours → Age >12 months: 30 ml/kg over 30 minutes, then 70 ml/kg over 2.5 hours
Mnemonic — The 30-70 Rule: 30 fast (bolus), then 70 slow (rehydration). The total is always 100 ml/kg — just split by severity of the initial phase.
The 3 Traps That Get Trainees in Trouble
Trap 1 — Wrong Plan B volume: ORS in Plan B is 75 ml/kg over 4 hours — not 50, not 100. Fix the number in your memory. Trap 2 — ORS via NGT in severe dehydration: If the child cannot drink → IV only. Nasogastric ORS is not equivalent to IV fluid in severe dehydration — do not use it as first step. Trap 3 — Cholera-like watery diarrhoea: A child may look "some dehydration" clinically but be losing fluid faster than you think. Reassess aggressively every 30–60 minutes in rice-water stool presentations.
Rule: After Plan C bolus, reassess after each phase. If the child can now drink and is improving → switch to ORS. If not improving → repeat the 30 ml/kg phase and consider other causes.
MedFlow Impression
ESS → Plan. 30 Fast, Then 70 Slow.
The three signs that push a child to Plan C are: skin pinch stays up, lethargic or unconscious, unable to drink. If any one is present — go straight to IV. The 30 ml/kg bolus is given fast for a reason. Don't negotiate the rate in a severely dehydrated child. And don't forget zinc — it reduces stool output and duration of illness in every child with diarrhoea regardless of plan.

Wednesday OB/GYN / Infectious Disease
Hook
HIV-positive woman. First antenatal visit at 10 weeks.She asks one question: "Will my baby be born negative?"The answer is yes — with a transmission risk under 2%.But only if you hit every checkpoint in the pathway. Miss one and the window closes.
HIV in Pregnancy — Screening Timeline & PMTCT (WHO 2021 / Option B+)
When to Screen — The 4 Windows
Window 1 — 1st ANC contact (any gestation): Test ALL women at first visit. This is not optional. Early diagnosis = longer time on ART before delivery = lower viral load at term. Window 2 — 28 weeks (2nd trimester retest): Retest all women who were negative at booking. Seroconversion can occur during pregnancy. In high-prevalence settings: retest at every trimester. Window 3 — 36 weeks (3rd trimester): Final retest. If positive or known positive: check viral load. VL >1000 copies/mL at 34–36 weeks → consider elective Caesarean section to reduce intrapartum transmission. Window 4 — Labour: Test any woman of unknown HIV status presenting in labour. Use rapid HIV test. If positive → start ART immediately and give infant NVP within 6 hours of delivery.
Rule: "No HIV status = test at every contact." No woman should deliver without a documented HIV result from that pregnancy.
ARV Regimen — Option B+ (Lifelong ART for All)
Start ART in ALL HIV-positive pregnant women regardless of CD4 count and gestational age. Do not wait. Preferred regimen (WHO 2021): TDF + 3TC + DTG (dolutegravir) → DTG preferred from the 2nd trimester onward. In the 1st trimester: use EFV if DTG is unavailable or there is a neural tube defect concern. → Continue lifelong after delivery — never stop (Option B+ principle). Viral load monitoring: at ART initiation, at 3 months, at 34–36 weeks, and 6 months postpartum. Mnemonic — TLD = Triple Lifelong Durability: TDF + 3TC + DTG. Three drugs. For life.
Rule: A suppressed viral load at delivery is the single most powerful intervention to prevent mother-to-child transmission. ART adherence from the first trimester onward achieves this.
Infant Prophylaxis + Feeding + PCR Timeline
Infant NVP (nevirapine): Start within 6 hours of birth (maximum 72 hours). → If mother on ART and VL suppressed: NVP for 6 weeks → If mother not on ART or VL not suppressed: NVP for 12 weeks (extended prophylaxis) Feeding: In resource-limited settings — exclusive breastfeeding + maternal ART throughout. Mixed feeding increases MTCT risk; avoid it. In well-resourced settings where safe alternatives are available: formula feeding. Infant PCR testing schedule: 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 6 months, 9 months, 18 months, and at cessation of breastfeeding. With optimal ART + NVP + correct feeding: MTCT risk <1–2%.
Rule: If a mother arrives in labour HIV-positive with no prior ART — start her ART immediately, give infant NVP for 12 weeks, and schedule infant PCR at 6 weeks. The clock starts at birth.
MedFlow Impression
PMTCT Failure Is Almost Always a Gap in the Pathway
Late booking. Interrupted ART. Unknown status in labour. Missed infant prophylaxis. Each one of those is a preventable transmission. Screen at 4 windows. Start TLD early. Check the viral load at 34–36 weeks. Give the infant NVP within 6 hours. Follow up with PCR at 6 weeks. A baby born to an HIV-positive mother on suppressive ART has a 98–99% chance of being negative. The pathway is the treatment.

Birth Asphyxia — The Role of CPAP
What CPAP Does — and Why It's the Right Step Here
CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) delivers constant pressure through both phases of the respiratory cycle — it does not breathe for the baby, but it holds the airways open. Mechanism: Prevents alveolar collapse at end-expiration → reduces atelectasis → improves V/Q matching → better oxygenation with less work of breathing. Appropriate for: spontaneously breathing infant with grunting, intercostal/subcostal recessions, SpO₂ <90% on free-flow O₂, tachypnoea >60, or post-resuscitation respiratory distress. NOT appropriate for: apnoea without respiratory drive (needs PPV/intubation), choanal atresia, tracheo-oesophageal fistula, untreated pneumothorax. Core rule: CPAP requires a breathing baby. If there is no respiratory drive — CPAP will not help. Move to PPV or intubation immediately.
Rule: CPAP is the bridge between free-flow oxygen and mechanical ventilation. It is not a minor step — a baby on CPAP is a sick baby who requires continuous monitoring.
Settings — Start Simple, Titrate Carefully
Starting pressure: 5 cmH₂O — do not start higher without a specific reason. Increase: by 1–2 cmH₂O increments if hypoxia persists. Maximum: typically 8 cmH₂O in neonates before considering escalation. FiO₂: Start at 0.3–0.4. Titrate to maintain SpO₂ 90–95%. → Target is NOT 100%. Hyperoxia causes retinopathy of prematurity and oxygen toxicity in neonates. Upper limit is 95%. Flow rate: typically 5–8 L/min (device-dependent — bubble CPAP or ventilator-derived CPAP). Interface: binasal prongs preferred over mask (better seal, less dead space).
Mnemonic — "CPAP at 5, SpO₂ at 95": Starting pressure is 5 cmH₂O. Target saturation ceiling is 95%. Two numbers. Locked in.
CPAP Failure — When to Escalate (The AFAR Criteria)
A — Apnoea on CPAP (repeated episodes despite correct settings → intubate) F — FiO₂ >0.6 required to maintain SpO₂ ≥90% (oxygen requirement too high for CPAP alone) A — Acidosis (respiratory acidosis on ABG — rising CO₂, pH falling, hypercapnia despite CPAP) R — Recessions worsening (increasing work of breathing, clinical exhaustion signs — infant tiring) If ANY one of these is present → escalate to intubation and mechanical ventilation. Do not wait for all four. One criterion is enough.
Mnemonic — AFAR: Apnoea · FiO₂ >0.6 · Acidosis · Recessions worsening = CPAP failure. Escalate early — a tired neonate decompensates faster than you expect.
MedFlow Impression
CPAP at 5. SpO₂ at 95. AFAR = Escalate.
After resuscitation for birth asphyxia, CPAP buys time and lung recruitment when the baby has a drive to breathe but the lungs are failing. Start at 5 cmH₂O. Target SpO₂ 90–95% — not higher. Reassess at 30 minutes. If AFAR criteria are met — escalate to intubation early, not as a last resort. Delayed intubation in a deteriorating neonate is the more dangerous choice. The ventilator is not a failure — it is the next rung on the ladder.
