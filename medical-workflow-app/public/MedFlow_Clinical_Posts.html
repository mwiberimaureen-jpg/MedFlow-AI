Monday Respiratory / Infectious
Hook
You treated "community-acquired pneumonia" with amoxicillin for 5 days.No improvement. Temperature still 38.9. Night sweats started.It was never pneumonia. The chest X-ray just looked that way.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation
Differential 1 — Pulmonary Tuberculosis
Sputum AFB x3, GeneXpert MTB/RIF, Mantoux/IGRA, CXR (upper lobe pattern, cavitation).
Rules in: positive AFB smear or GeneXpert. Rules out: negative GeneXpert + negative culture at 6 weeks (with low pre-test probability).

CT chest (hilar/mediastinal adenopathy), LDH, serum protein electrophoresis, bronchoscopy with BAL and biopsy.
Rules in: hilar adenopathy + histological confirmation on biopsy. Rules out: normal CT and LDH with alternative diagnosis confirmed.
Differential 3 — Pulmonary Vasculitis (GPA)
ANCA (PR3/MPO), urine dipstick for haematuria, CT chest (cavitating nodules), renal function.
Rules in: positive PR3-ANCA + cavitating lesions + renal involvement + biopsy. Rules out: negative ANCA with alternative diagnosis confirmed.
MedFlow Impression
Pulmonary Tuberculosis
Antibiotic failure at 5 days + night sweats + upper lobe pattern = TB until proven otherwise. Isolate the patient. Initiate sputum collection. Refer to infectious disease before changing antibiotics empirically.

Diuretic-Induced Hyponatraemia

BNP or NT-proBNP, CXR (pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines), ECG, echocardiogram.

Acute Decompensated Cardiac Failure
Fluid-responsive dehydration improves with fluid. If the patient deteriorates after 2L of saline, the diagnosis was wrong. Check the BNP. Get the CXR. The fluid may have just tipped her into pulmonary oedema.

Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
Distal duodenal ulcer + multiple simultaneous ulcers + PPI failure = gastrinoma until excluded. Do not scope and discharge. Check the fasting gastrin. If elevated, the tumour needs to be found — it may be malignant.

Tuesday Surgery / Post-operative
Hook
Post-op day 2. Patient spiked a fever of 38.4°C.You start antibiotics.The fever on post-op day 2 is almost never infection. You just treated the wrong thing.
Here's what MedFlow offered — The 5 W's of Post-operative Fever
Wind — Days 1–2 (Atelectasis)
Clinical: decreased air entry, dullness at bases. CXR. Incentive spirometry, chest physiotherapy, early mobilisation.
Rules in: clinical + CXR findings. Treatment: physiotherapy, NOT antibiotics.
Water — Days 3–5 (UTI)
Urine dipstick, urine MC&S. Common if urinary catheter in situ.
Rules in: positive nitrites/leucocytes on dipstick + positive MC&S. Treat per sensitivities.
Wound — Days 5–7 (Surgical Site Infection)
Wound inspection (erythema, induration, discharge), wound swab MC&S.
Rules in: clinical signs of wound infection + positive swab culture.
Walking — Days 5+ (DVT / PE)
D-dimer, USS doppler lower limbs, CTPA if PE suspected. Wells score to guide investigation.
Rules in: positive CTPA or USS. Anticoagulate immediately if confirmed.
Wonder Drugs — Any Day (Drug Fever)
Review drug chart. Withdrawal of offending agent. Diagnosis of exclusion.
Rules in: fever resolves within 72 hours of drug withdrawal. Rules out: fever persists after withdrawal.
MedFlow Impression
Post-operative Atelectasis (Day 2)
Day 2 post-op fever is atelectasis until proven otherwise. Encourage deep breathing and early ambulation. Hold antibiotics unless a specific infectious source is identified. Starting antibiotics reflexively on day 2 fever is one of the most common errors on surgical wards.

Thursday Haematology
Hook
Patient's urine turned dark brown after an acute illness.LFTs: normal. Bilirubin: normal. Urobilinogen: elevated.The liver is not the problem. Something is happening upstream of it.
Here's what MedFlow offered in this situation

FBC + blood film (spherocytes, schistocytes, polychromasia), reticulocyte count, LDH, haptoglobin (low in haemolysis), direct Coombs test (DAT), bilirubin fractions (unconjugated elevated).

Serum CK (markedly elevated), urine myoglobin, UECs (acute kidney injury — the main complication), urine dipstick (blood positive, no RBCs on microscopy).

Haemolytic Anaemia — Query G6PD Deficiency
Dark urine after acute illness with normal LFTs = red cell destruction, not liver disease. Elevated urobilinogen confirms the bilirubin load is coming from haemolysis. Check the film. Check the enzyme — but wait until the acute episode settles for an accurate result.

Monday Paediatrics / Neurology
Hook
You gave the anticonvulsant. The seizure stopped.You wrote "febrile convulsion" and planned discharge.Her capillary blood glucose was 1.2 mmol/L. Nobody had checked.She was back 4 hours later — seizing again. The fever was never the cause.
Convulsions in Children — Classification of Causes
Metabolic Causes — Remember MATCH
M — Magnesium low (hypomagnesaemia — neonates, malnutrition) A — Ammonia high (inborn errors of metabolism, urea cycle defects) T — Timber! Glucose low (hypoglycaemia — the most common missed metabolic cause; check BGL in every seizing child) C — Calcium low (hypocalcaemia — neonates, vitamin D deficiency, hypoparathyroidism) H — Hyponatraemia (Na <125 mmol/L — dilutional, gastroenteritis with hypotonic fluids) First-line bedside tests: capillary blood glucose, Na, Ca, Mg.
Rule: "Febrile convulsion" is a diagnosis of exclusion. Check the glucose before you use that label — every time, without exception.
Infectious Causes — Remember BEAM
B — Bacterial meningitis (fever + seizure + neck stiffness or bulging fontanelle → LP after stabilisation; do NOT delay antibiotics for imaging in a sick child) E — Encephalitis (viral — HSV most important; altered consciousness + personality change + fever → aciclovir empirically while awaiting PCR) A — Abscess (cerebral — subacute onset, focal neurological signs, raised ICP → CT with contrast first) M — Malaria (cerebral malaria — P. falciparum; any febrile child with seizures in an endemic region → thick & thin blood film immediately) Key investigations: LP (if safe), blood cultures, thick film, HSV PCR on CSF.
Rule: In a child under 18 months, absence of neck stiffness does NOT exclude bacterial meningitis. LP is mandatory if no contraindications.
Structural / Epileptic Causes
Epilepsy (first unprovoked afebrile seizure → EEG + MRI brain) Neurocysticercosis (ring-enhancing lesions on CT — endemic area, exposure to undercooked pork; serology + CT head) Space-occupying lesion / vascular malformation (focal onset, focal signs → urgent CT) Hypoxic-ischaemic injury (perinatal history — relevant in neonates) CT head indications: focal signs, prolonged seizure (>30 min), first unprovoked seizure, no metabolic or infectious cause identified.
Rule: A normal CT does not rule out epilepsy — EEG is the key investigation for epilepsy, not imaging.
MedFlow Impression
Every Seizing Child Gets a BGL and Na — Before Any Other Label
MATCH the metabolic causes. BEAM through the infectious ones. "Febrile convulsion" only applies to a child over 6 months with a simple, brief, generalised seizure who has no signs of CNS infection and a normal BGL and electrolytes. Before you write it — prove it. One missed glucose is one preventable readmission.

Tuesday Paediatrics / Emergency
Hook
18-month-old. 3 days of watery diarrhoea. Sunken eyes. Crying without tears.Skin pinch stays up for 2 seconds. Pulse rapid and weak. Lethargic.You know he needs fluids. But which plan — and at exactly what rate?Most trainees give the right fluid. They give it at the wrong speed.
Dehydration in Paediatrics — WHO/IMCI Plan A, B, C
Step 1 — Assess Severity: The ESS Signs (Eyes, Skin, State)
No dehydration: Alert, normal eyes, normal skin turgor, drinks normally → Plan A Some dehydration (5–10%): Restless/irritable, sunken eyes, skin pinch returns slowly (1–2 sec), drinks eagerly → Plan B Severe dehydration (>10%): Lethargic or unconscious, very sunken eyes, skin pinch stays up >2 seconds, unable to drink, weak or absent pulse → Plan C One of these three signs = severe: skin stays up, lethargic, unable to drink.
Mnemonic — ESS: Eyes · Skin · State. If one of the "severe" ESS signs is present, go straight to Plan C. Do not use the oral route in severe dehydration.
Plan A, B, C — The ORS Ladder
Plan A (No dehydration — prevention at home): → ORS 10 ml/kg after each loose stool. Continue breastfeeding and normal feeds. Zinc 20 mg/day for 10 days (<6 months: 10 mg/day). Plan B (Some dehydration — ORS in facility): → ORS 75 ml/kg over 4 hours, given in small sips. Reassess every 1–2 hours. Improving → continue. Worsening → escalate to Plan C. Plan C (Severe dehydration — IV fluids): → IV Ringer's Lactate (preferred) or Normal Saline → Age ≤12 months: 30 ml/kg over 1 hour, then 70 ml/kg over 5 hours → Age >12 months: 30 ml/kg over 30 minutes, then 70 ml/kg over 2.5 hours
Mnemonic — The 30-70 Rule: 30 fast (bolus), then 70 slow (rehydration). The total is always 100 ml/kg — just split by severity of the initial phase.
The 3 Traps That Get Trainees in Trouble
Trap 1 — Wrong Plan B volume: ORS in Plan B is 75 ml/kg over 4 hours — not 50, not 100. Fix the number in your memory. Trap 2 — ORS via NGT in severe dehydration: If the child cannot drink → IV only. Nasogastric ORS is not equivalent to IV fluid in severe dehydration — do not use it as first step. Trap 3 — Cholera-like watery diarrhoea: A child may look "some dehydration" clinically but be losing fluid faster than you think. Reassess aggressively every 30–60 minutes in rice-water stool presentations.
Rule: After Plan C bolus, reassess after each phase. If the child can now drink and is improving → switch to ORS. If not improving → repeat the 30 ml/kg phase and consider other causes.
MedFlow Impression
ESS → Plan. 30 Fast, Then 70 Slow.
The three signs that push a child to Plan C are: skin pinch stays up, lethargic or unconscious, unable to drink. If any one is present — go straight to IV. The 30 ml/kg bolus is given fast for a reason. Don't negotiate the rate in a severely dehydrated child. And don't forget zinc — it reduces stool output and duration of illness in every child with diarrhoea regardless of plan.

Wednesday OB/GYN / Infectious Disease
Hook
HIV-positive woman. First antenatal visit at 10 weeks.She asks one question: "Will my baby be born negative?"The answer is yes — with a transmission risk under 2%.But only if you hit every checkpoint in the pathway. Miss one and the window closes.
HIV in Pregnancy — Screening Timeline & PMTCT (WHO 2021 / Option B+)
When to Screen — The 4 Windows
Window 1 — 1st ANC contact (any gestation): Test ALL women at first visit. This is not optional. Early diagnosis = longer time on ART before delivery = lower viral load at term. Window 2 — 28 weeks (2nd trimester retest): Retest all women who were negative at booking. Seroconversion can occur during pregnancy. In high-prevalence settings: retest at every trimester. Window 3 — 36 weeks (3rd trimester): Final retest. If positive or known positive: check viral load. VL >1000 copies/mL at 34–36 weeks → consider elective Caesarean section to reduce intrapartum transmission. Window 4 — Labour: Test any woman of unknown HIV status presenting in labour. Use rapid HIV test. If positive → start ART immediately and give infant NVP within 6 hours of delivery.
Rule: "No HIV status = test at every contact." No woman should deliver without a documented HIV result from that pregnancy.
ARV Regimen — Option B+ (Lifelong ART for All)
Start ART in ALL HIV-positive pregnant women regardless of CD4 count and gestational age. Do not wait. Preferred regimen (WHO 2021): TDF + 3TC + DTG (dolutegravir) → DTG preferred from the 2nd trimester onward. In the 1st trimester: use EFV if DTG is unavailable or there is a neural tube defect concern. → Continue lifelong after delivery — never stop (Option B+ principle). Viral load monitoring: at ART initiation, at 3 months, at 34–36 weeks, and 6 months postpartum. Mnemonic — TLD = Triple Lifelong Durability: TDF + 3TC + DTG. Three drugs. For life.
Rule: A suppressed viral load at delivery is the single most powerful intervention to prevent mother-to-child transmission. ART adherence from the first trimester onward achieves this.
Infant Prophylaxis + Feeding + PCR Timeline
Infant NVP (nevirapine): Start within 6 hours of birth (maximum 72 hours). → If mother on ART and VL suppressed: NVP for 6 weeks → If mother not on ART or VL not suppressed: NVP for 12 weeks (extended prophylaxis) Feeding: In resource-limited settings — exclusive breastfeeding + maternal ART throughout. Mixed feeding increases MTCT risk; avoid it. In well-resourced settings where safe alternatives are available: formula feeding. Infant PCR testing schedule: 6 weeks, 10 weeks, 6 months, 9 months, 18 months, and at cessation of breastfeeding. With optimal ART + NVP + correct feeding: MTCT risk <1–2%.
Rule: If a mother arrives in labour HIV-positive with no prior ART — start her ART immediately, give infant NVP for 12 weeks, and schedule infant PCR at 6 weeks. The clock starts at birth.
MedFlow Impression
PMTCT Failure Is Almost Always a Gap in the Pathway
Late booking. Interrupted ART. Unknown status in labour. Missed infant prophylaxis. Each one of those is a preventable transmission. Screen at 4 windows. Start TLD early. Check the viral load at 34–36 weeks. Give the infant NVP within 6 hours. Follow up with PCR at 6 weeks. A baby born to an HIV-positive mother on suppressive ART has a 98–99% chance of being negative. The pathway is the treatment.

Birth Asphyxia — The Role of CPAP
What CPAP Does — and Why It's the Right Step Here
CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) delivers constant pressure through both phases of the respiratory cycle — it does not breathe for the baby, but it holds the airways open. Mechanism: Prevents alveolar collapse at end-expiration → reduces atelectasis → improves V/Q matching → better oxygenation with less work of breathing. Appropriate for: spontaneously breathing infant with grunting, intercostal/subcostal recessions, SpO₂ <90% on free-flow O₂, tachypnoea >60, or post-resuscitation respiratory distress. NOT appropriate for: apnoea without respiratory drive (needs PPV/intubation), choanal atresia, tracheo-oesophageal fistula, untreated pneumothorax. Core rule: CPAP requires a breathing baby. If there is no respiratory drive — CPAP will not help. Move to PPV or intubation immediately.
Rule: CPAP is the bridge between free-flow oxygen and mechanical ventilation. It is not a minor step — a baby on CPAP is a sick baby who requires continuous monitoring.
Settings — Start Simple, Titrate Carefully
Starting pressure: 5 cmH₂O — do not start higher without a specific reason. Increase: by 1–2 cmH₂O increments if hypoxia persists. Maximum: typically 8 cmH₂O in neonates before considering escalation. FiO₂: Start at 0.3–0.4. Titrate to maintain SpO₂ 90–95%. → Target is NOT 100%. Hyperoxia causes retinopathy of prematurity and oxygen toxicity in neonates. Upper limit is 95%. Flow rate: typically 5–8 L/min (device-dependent — bubble CPAP or ventilator-derived CPAP). Interface: binasal prongs preferred over mask (better seal, less dead space).
Mnemonic — "CPAP at 5, SpO₂ at 95": Starting pressure is 5 cmH₂O. Target saturation ceiling is 95%. Two numbers. Locked in.
CPAP Failure — When to Escalate (The AFAR Criteria)
A — Apnoea on CPAP (repeated episodes despite correct settings → intubate) F — FiO₂ >0.6 required to maintain SpO₂ ≥90% (oxygen requirement too high for CPAP alone) A — Acidosis (respiratory acidosis on ABG — rising CO₂, pH falling, hypercapnia despite CPAP) R — Recessions worsening (increasing work of breathing, clinical exhaustion signs — infant tiring) If ANY one of these is present → escalate to intubation and mechanical ventilation. Do not wait for all four. One criterion is enough.
Mnemonic — AFAR: Apnoea · FiO₂ >0.6 · Acidosis · Recessions worsening = CPAP failure. Escalate early — a tired neonate decompensates faster than you expect.
MedFlow Impression
CPAP at 5. SpO₂ at 95. AFAR = Escalate.
After resuscitation for birth asphyxia, CPAP buys time and lung recruitment when the baby has a drive to breathe but the lungs are failing. Start at 5 cmH₂O. Target SpO₂ 90–95% — not higher. Reassess at 30 minutes. If AFAR criteria are met — escalate to intubation early, not as a last resort. Delayed intubation in a deteriorating neonate is the more dangerous choice. The ventilator is not a failure — it is the next rung on the ladder.

Hypernatraemic Dehydration — Why Fast Correction Kills
What High Sodium Does to the Brain — and Why It Fights Back
High serum sodium pulls water OUT of cells by osmosis — including brain cells. The brain shrinks. This is dangerous. The brain's counter-move: it generates intracellular osmoles (called idiogenic osmoles or organic osmolytes — taurine, myoinositol, glutamine) to pull water back in. Over 24–48 hours, brain cell volume is restored. The trap: the brain is now packed with osmolytes. If you drop the sodium too fast, water floods into brain cells before osmolytes can be cleared → cells swell → cerebral oedema → seizures → herniation. The brain has adapted to the high sodium. It has not adapted to a rapid drop. Clinical clues to hypernatraemia (before the lab comes back): → Skin has a "doughy" texture — different from normal dehydration pinch → Irritability and high-pitched cry out of proportion to the clinical state → Neurological signs: lethargy, hypotonia, opisthotonus in severe cases
Rule: Hypernatraemia is corrected over 48–72 hours, not 4–6 hours. The deficit replacement rate is determined by the sodium — not by the severity of dehydration alone.
How to Correct Safely — The 10–12 Rule
Maximum rate of sodium correction: 10–12 mmol/L per 24 hours (approximately 0.5 mmol/L per hour). Do NOT target faster correction than this, even if the sodium is severely elevated. Fluid strategy: → STEP 1 — If haemodynamically compromised (shocked): give 0.9% NaCl 10–20 ml/kg as a bolus first. Restoring perfusion takes priority over sodium correction speed. → STEP 2 — Deficit replacement: use 0.45% NaCl (hypotonic) or calculate the free water deficit and replace gradually over 48–72 hours. → Monitor Na every 4–6 hours during correction and adjust rate. Free water deficit formula (rough guide): → Free water deficit (L) = 4 ml/kg × weight (kg) × (Na measured − Na target [145]) Ongoing losses: replace with 0.45% NaCl or ORS concurrently if diarrhoea continues.
Mnemonic — "10 in 24": Drop the sodium by no more than 10 mmol/L per 24 hours. Write it on the fluid order. Tell the nurses. Set a timer for the 4-hour Na recheck.
The Common Presentation — Breastfeeding Hypernatraemia
The most common cause of hypernatraemic dehydration in neonates: → Breastfeeding failure in the first week of life — inadequate milk supply, poor latch, or delayed feeding establishment → Baby loses >10% birth weight, becomes lethargic, feeds poorly → Na can reach 155–175 mmol/L within 5–7 days Why it's missed: → Baby appears "sleepy and content" — actually profoundly dehydrated → Parents are reassured by peaceful appearance → Weight not checked after discharge Management: → Supplement breastfeeding with expressed breast milk or formula immediately → Correct sodium slowly as above — neonates are especially vulnerable to cerebral oedema → Lactation consultant input for long-term breastfeeding support
Rule: A sleepy newborn at day 5–7 who has lost >10% birth weight is not "doing well on breastfeeding." Check the sodium before reassuring the parents.
MedFlow Impression

The Plan C fluid rate is designed for isonatraemic dehydration. If the sodium is above 150, that rate is dangerous. The haemodynamic bolus is still appropriate — restore the circulation first. Check it before you write the fluid order.

ORS — The Science Behind the Sachet
The Mechanism — SGLT1 Is Why It Works
Cholera toxin forces chloride channels open permanently → sodium, chloride, and water pour into the gut lumen (secretory diarrhoea). This process cannot be switched off. BUT: the sodium-glucose cotransporter (SGLT1) in the enterocyte wall remains fully functional. SGLT1's rule: it will actively transport glucose AND sodium together into the cell — only when BOTH are present simultaneously. When glucose is in the gut lumen: → SGLT1 pulls glucose + Na into the enterocyte → Water follows osmotically → Net absorption occurs even during active secretory diarrhoea ORS is not a fluid replacement — it is a pharmacological exploit of SGLT1. The glucose is not food. The salt is not seasoning. Together they are the mechanism.
Rule: ORS works because of the glucose-sodium cotransporter — not glucose alone, not salt alone. The ratio is the medicine.
WHO Low-Osmolarity ORS Composition — Know the Numbers
Sodium: 75 mmol/L Glucose: 75 mmol/L (ratio to Na ≈ 1:1 — critical for cotransport) Chloride: 65 mmol/L Potassium: 20 mmol/L (replaces stool losses — prevents hypokalaemia) Citrate: 10 mmol/L (base to correct acidosis) Total osmolarity: 245 mOsm/L Why low osmolarity matters: → The original 1975 WHO formula had osmolarity of 311 mOsm/L → The 2002 reduced-osmolarity formula (245 mOsm/L) reduces stool output by 20–25% and reduces need for IV supplementation → Lower osmolarity = less osmotic load in the gut = better net absorption
Mnemonic — "75-75": Sodium 75, Glucose 75. The 1:1 ratio is the active ingredient. Everything else is supporting chemistry.

Rule: ORS is a drug. Substitutes that get the glucose:sodium ratio wrong don't just fail — they can actively worsen dehydration. Teach this to families before you discharge them.

Early Infant Diagnosis — Why Antibody Tests Fail in Infants, and What to Use Instead
Why the Rapid HIV Test Is Wrong in Any Infant Under 18 Months
Standard HIV tests (rapid antibody tests, ELISA, Western blot) detect HIV ANTIBODIES — not the virus itself. The problem: maternal HIV antibodies cross the placenta freely during pregnancy and persist in infant blood for 12–18 months. This means: → A rapid HIV antibody test will be POSITIVE in EVERY HIV-exposed infant until 12–18 months of age — regardless of whether the baby is actually infected. → A negative rapid test before 18 months also cannot confidently exclude infection in a recently exposed infant. The solution: HIV DNA PCR (qualitative PCR) → Detects HIV proviral DNA integrated into the infant's own CD4 T-cells → Not affected by maternal antibodies → Can detect infection as early as 2–4 weeks (but 6 weeks is the standard first test)
Rule: Never use a rapid HIV antibody test to diagnose or exclude HIV infection in a child under 18 months. The test measures the mother's immune response — not the baby's infection status.
Early Infant Diagnosis (EID) Schedule — When to Test
6 weeks (First PCR): → The earliest standardly reliable test. Send as part of the routine 6-week visit. → A positive result strongly suggests infection — confirm with repeat PCR within 4 weeks. → If symptomatic and positive: start ART immediately without waiting for confirmation. 10 weeks (Second PCR if 6-week positive): → Confirmatory test. Positive on two PCRs = confirmed HIV infection → start ART now. 6 months (Third PCR): → Routine follow-up. Negative here + non-breastfeeding = infection unlikely but not excluded. At cessation of breastfeeding AND at 3 months post-cessation: → Breastfeeding continues to transmit HIV. Each feed is a new exposure event. → Do not declare the baby HIV-negative until 3 months after the last breastfeed. 18 months (Final confirmatory test): → Maternal antibodies have cleared → HIV antibody test is now interpretable. → Negative at 18 months + no breastfeeding = definitive HIV-negative.
Mnemonic — 6, 10, 6m, Cessation +3m, 18m: these are the 5 PCR/antibody checkpoints for every HIV-exposed infant.
Interpreting a Positive PCR — What to Do Next
Positive PCR at 6 weeks: → Repeat PCR within 2–4 weeks (confirmatory sample) — the "first positive, second positive" rule → If symptomatic (failure to thrive, recurrent infections, oral thrush): do NOT wait for confirmation → start ART immediately → If asymptomatic: wait for confirmation before starting ART Confirmed positive (2 positive PCRs): → Start ART immediately — all HIV-positive children under 5 years start ART regardless of CD4 count → Refer to paediatric HIV programme → Ensure cotrimoxazole prophylaxis is already running (should have been started at 6 weeks) False positive PCR: rare but occurs — always confirm with a second independent sample on a second blood draw. Do not start lifelong ART on one result alone in an asymptomatic infant.
Rule: One positive PCR = confirm. Two positive PCRs = start ART. One negative PCR + breastfeeding = continue testing. HIV-free status is declared only 3 months after the last breastfeed.

Rule: In children under 18 months, "wait and see" kills. There is no clinical scenario in which an HIV-positive child should not be on ART. Start it.

Birth Asphyxia — The Golden Minute
The First 30 Seconds — WADS
W — Warm: Dry immediately and place under a radiant warmer. Remove wet towels and replace. Hypothermia in an asphyxiated neonate doubles mortality — cold is the enemy. A — Airway: Position the head in neutral "sniffing" position. Suction only if secretions or meconium are visible at the mouth or nose. Avoid deep suction — it causes bradycardia and laryngospasm. D — Dry and Stimulate: Dry vigorously with a warm towel. Rub the back and soles of the feet firmly. This is adequate stimulation for the vast majority of neonates. S — Simultaneously Assess: Is the baby breathing? What is the heart rate? (Count for 6 seconds and multiply by 10, or feel the umbilical cord pulse) Mnemonic — WADS: Warm · Airway · Dry/Stimulate · Assess. In that order.
Rule: Drying and warming happen simultaneously with airway positioning. They are not sequential — you are doing all of this at once in the first 30 seconds.
30 Seconds to 60 Seconds — If Not Breathing or HR <100
If baby is not breathing adequately OR heart rate <100 bpm after 30 seconds of WADS: → START POSITIVE PRESSURE VENTILATION (PPV) → Rate: 40–60 breaths per minute ("breathe-2-3, breathe-2-3") → FiO₂: Room air (21%) for TERM infants — do NOT start with 100% oxygen → Pressure: 20–25 cmH₂O for initial breaths; 15–20 cmH₂O for maintenance → Check: mask seal is tight, chest rises with each breath, breath sounds audible At 60 seconds (re-assess): → HR ≥100 + breathing: continue supportive care, wean O₂ → HR 60–99: continue PPV, adjust technique, consider increasing FiO₂ → HR <60 despite 30 seconds of effective PPV: start chest compressions immediately (3:1 ratio of compressions to ventilations)
Rule: Start with room air in term infants. The landmark NRP/WHO evidence shows worse neurological outcomes with 100% O₂ at birth. Titrate FiO₂ up only if SpO₂ remains below target despite effective PPV.
Common Errors in the Golden Minute — What to Unlearn
Error 1 — Suctioning before drying: No evidence of benefit in non-meconium deliveries. Costs time. Causes bradycardia. Dry first — always. Error 2 — Starting with 100% O₂: Multiple RCTs show worse neurological outcomes and higher mortality with 100% O₂ vs room air at birth in term neonates. Oxygen is a drug. Titrate it. Error 3 — Delayed cord clamping in a compromised infant: Delayed clamping (30–60 seconds) is recommended for healthy term and preterm infants. If the baby needs immediate resuscitation at the warmer → clamp now, move fast. Error 4 — Waiting for the 1-minute Apgar before acting: The Apgar score is a retrospective documentation tool. If the baby is not breathing, start PPV — do not wait 1 minute to confirm with a score. Error 5 — Trying to resuscitate alone: Call for help before 60 seconds. Chest compressions + ventilation require two people. A solo resuscitator will be compromised at that point.
Rule: The Apgar score does not guide resuscitation. Your assessment of breathing and heart rate does. Act on those — the Apgar gets filled in afterward.
MedFlow Impression
WADS. Room Air. Act at 30 Seconds. Call for Help Before 60.
The golden minute protocol works — but only if the sequence is correct and the timing is adhered to. The single most common error is suction before drying (costs 10 seconds, no benefit). The most consequential error is 100% O₂ in a term infant (evidence of harm). Know your weight-adjusted PPV pressures before you enter the delivery room. Know your team roles. Neonatal resuscitation is not improvised — every second is protocol.

Hypoxic-Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE) — Sarnat Staging
Sarnat Grades — Mild, Moderate, Severe
Grade 1 — Mild HIE: → Hyperalert, irritable, pupils dilated, tone mildly increased, mild feeding difficulty → Deep tendon reflexes brisk, Moro exaggerated → EEG: normal or mildly abnormal → Duration: resolves completely within 24–48 hours → Prognosis: excellent — normal neurodevelopmental outcome in the vast majority → Management: supportive. Do NOT cool. Grade 2 — Moderate HIE: → Lethargic, hypotonic, abnormal primitive reflexes (weak or absent Moro, poor grasp, absent suck) → Seizures — typically onset at 12–24 hours of age → EEG: abnormal — burst suppression or low voltage → Duration: abnormalities persist >7 days → Prognosis: 25–30% have significant disability; 10–20% die → Management: THERAPEUTIC HYPOTHERMIA if within 6 hours of birth Grade 3 — Severe HIE: → Comatose, flaccid, absent brainstem reflexes (absent pupillary response, absent oculocephalic reflex) → Prolonged, poorly controlled seizures; apnoea → EEG: burst suppression pattern or flat (isoelectric) trace → Prognosis: majority die or survive with severe disability (cerebral palsy, intellectual disability, epilepsy) → Management: cooling if within 6 hours (benefit less certain than grade 2); intensive care; family counselling
Mnemonic — "Irritable → Lethargic → Comatose": Mild Grade 1 → Moderate Grade 2 → Severe Grade 3. The grade at 6 hours is the most predictive. Document it every time.
The Thompson Score — Bedside Staging Without a Neurologist
The Thompson score allows bedside HIE grading using 9 clinical parameters scored 0–3 each: Tone · Level of consciousness · Fits (seizures) · Posture · Moro reflex · Grasp · Suck · Pupils · Respiration Total score: 0–22 → Thompson score <7: likely mild HIE (Grade 1) — monitor → Thompson score ≥7 at 4–6 hours: moderate or severe HIE — consider therapeutic hypothermia Why the Thompson score matters: → Full Sarnat classification requires detailed neurological examination — takes time → Thompson can be completed by any clinician at the bedside → Reproducible between observers → Validated threshold for cooling decisions in resource-limited settings
Rule: If you cannot complete a full Sarnat exam, use the Thompson score. A Thompson ≥7 at 4–6 hours of age is the practical threshold for initiating cooling discussion.
The 6-Hour Window — Why Staging at 4–6 Hours Is Critical
After birth asphyxia, brain injury occurs in two phases: Phase 1 — Primary energy failure (0–6 hours): → Neurons deplete ATP → immediate cell death. Largely irreversible. Nothing stops this phase. Phase 2 — Secondary energy failure (6–72 hours): → Reperfusion injury + glutamate excitotoxicity + oxidative stress → apoptotic cascade → THIS IS REVERSIBLE → This is exactly what therapeutic hypothermia targets. The window: therapeutic hypothermia must be started WITHIN 6 HOURS of birth. → After 6 hours: the secondary cascade is already underway — cooling no longer alters the trajectory → Assessment at 4–6 hours is the last clinical decision point before the window closes Grade 1 at 6 hours: observe, no cooling, reassess at 24 hours Grade 2 at 6 hours: cool — within the window — this is where outcome is modifiable Grade 3 at 6 hours: cool if possible, recognise guarded prognosis regardless
Rule: Staging at birth is insufficient — grade at 6 hours. An infant who appears mildly affected at birth can evolve to Grade 2 by 6 hours as the full picture emerges. The 6-hour grade drives the cooling decision.

Therapeutic Hypothermia — Who, How, and What to Do Without Equipment
What Cooling Does to the Injured Brain
After birth asphyxia, the brain's secondary injury cascade (6–72 hours) involves: → Glutamate excitotoxicity — excess glutamate overstimulates NMDA receptors → calcium influx → mitochondrial failure → Oxygen free radical production — oxidative damage to cell membranes → Inflammatory cascade — microglia activation, cytokine release → Apoptosis — programmed cell death in vulnerable neurones What hypothermia at 33–34°C does: → Reduces cerebral metabolic rate by 25–30% (less ATP needed, less failure) → Suppresses glutamate release → Reduces free radical production → Inhibits apoptotic signalling pathways Outcome evidence: → Reduces combined outcome of death or major neurodisability by approximately 25% in moderate HIE → Number needed to treat: approximately 7 (for one additional baby to survive without disability) → Effect is greatest in Grade 2 HIE. Grade 3: less certain benefit, but cooling is still offered.
Rule: Cooling does not reverse Phase 1 injury. It specifically targets Phase 2 (secondary energy failure). This is why the 6-hour window exists — Phase 2 begins then.
Eligibility Criteria — All Must Be Present
1. Gestational age ≥36 weeks (preterm <36 weeks: cooling may cause harm — evidence insufficient) 2. Birth weight ≥1800 g (proxy for ≥36 weeks gestation) 3. Age ≤6 hours from delivery 4. Evidence of perinatal asphyxia — at least ONE of: → Apgar ≤5 at 10 minutes → Continued need for resuscitation at 10 minutes → Umbilical cord/early blood gas pH <7.0 OR base excess ≤−16 mmol/L → Acute sentinel event (uterine rupture, cord prolapse, severe bradycardia) 5. PLUS clinical evidence of moderate or severe HIE (Sarnat grade 2 or 3, or Thompson ≥7) Exclusion: → Major congenital anomaly incompatible with life → Life-threatening coagulopathy → Severe growth restriction (<1800g regardless of gestation)
Rule: All 5 criteria must be present. If the baby is ≥36 weeks, has a cord gas pH <7.0, and has a Thompson ≥7 — start the cooling conversation immediately. You have 6 hours from birth, not from diagnosis.
When There Is No Cooling Machine — Passive Cooling Protocol
Servo-controlled cooling (standard care): specialised mattress or blanket maintains rectal temperature at 33–34°C for exactly 72 hours. Available in NICU/tertiary settings. When equipment is unavailable — "Passive" or improvised cooling: → Remove all clothing and blankets from the baby → Turn OFF the radiant warmer → Place in a room at ambient temperature (approximately 22–24°C) → Monitor temperature every 15–30 minutes with a rectal probe (target 33–34°C) → RISK: passive cooling is imprecise — overcooling (<33°C) causes cardiac arrhythmias and coagulopathy; undercooling (>34°C) reduces efficacy → Use passive cooling as a BRIDGE during transfer — not as definitive treatment if a cooling centre is reachable within the window During any cooling (protocol): → Monitor: blood glucose every 1–2 hours (hypothermia causes hypoglycaemia), Na, Ca, coagulation → EEG monitoring: subclinical seizures are common in HIE — treat with phenobarbitone → Maintain normal glucose, electrolytes, blood pressure throughout Rewarming: after 72 hours, rewarm SLOWLY at 0.2–0.5°C per hour. Rapid rewarming = seizure risk.
Mnemonic — "33 for 72, then warm slowly": Target temperature 33–34°C. Duration 72 hours. Rewarm at <0.5°C/hour. Three numbers to remember before your patient needs them.
