PPH — The 4 T's and HAEMOSTASIS
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
Your patient is a 28-year-old G3P2 who just delivered vaginally. Estimated blood loss is 650ml and rising. The uterus is boggy on palpation. She is tachycardic. You have 2 minutes to have a plan.
2
Slide 2 — The 4 T's: Find the Cause First
T
Tone — Uterine atony. 70% of PPH cases. The uterus is not contracting.
T
Trauma — Lacerations, uterine rupture, haematoma. Inspect the entire birth canal.
T
Tissue — Retained placenta or products. Is the placenta complete?
T
Thrombin — Coagulopathy. DIC, pre-existing bleeding disorder.
Key point: Identify the T before you treat. The intervention depends entirely on the cause.
3
Slide 3 — HAEMOSTASIS Steps 1–5
H
Help — Call your senior now. Not when she is in shock.
A
Assess and resuscitate — IV access x2, FBC, coagulation, crossmatch, urinary catheter.
E
Establish cause — Which T?
M
Massage — Bimanual uterine compression while preparing oxytocics.
O
Oxytocin — 10 units IM/IV + Ergometrine 0.5mg IM (avoid if hypertensive). Tranexamic acid 1g IV within 3 hours.
4
Slide 4 — HAEMOSTASIS Steps 6–9
S
Shift to theatre — If medical management fails.
T
Tamponade — Bakri balloon or condom catheter with 300–500ml saline.
A
Apply compression sutures — B-Lynch suture.
S
Systematic devascularisation — Uterine artery ligation.
I
Interventional radiology — Uterine artery embolization if available.
S
Subtotal/total hysterectomy — Last resort. Life-saving.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Call for help at 500ml — not when she is in shock. Tranexamic acid reduces mortality when given within 3 hours of delivery. If the uterus remains boggy after massage and oxytocics, move to tamponade without delay. Every minute in a PPH is a minute closer to DIC. (CTA)

Mnemonics · Know Your Management · 1 day
Heart Failure — LMNOP and ABCDD
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
Your patient is a 65-year-old hypertensive man with acute dyspnoea, orthopnoea, bilateral leg oedema, and basal crackles. BP 180/110, SpO2 88% on room air. He is in acute decompensated heart failure. What is your immediate management?
2
Slide 2 — LMNOP: Acute Heart Failure
L
Lasix (Furosemide) — 40–80mg IV. Reduces preload and fluid overload.
M
Morphine — 2–4mg IV (use cautiously — respiratory depression risk). Reduces anxiety and preload.
N
Nitrates — GTN sublingual or IV infusion. Avoid if systolic BP <90.
O
Oxygen — Target SpO2 94–98%. Use CPAP/NIV if not improving on O2 alone.
P
Position — Sit the patient upright immediately. Reduces venous return.
Key point: Position and oxygen cost nothing and start in seconds.
3
Slide 3 — ABCDD: Chronic Heart Failure (HFrEF)
A
ACE inhibitor (or ARB if intolerant) — Reduces mortality. Start low, titrate up.
B
Beta-blocker (carvedilol, bisoprolol) — Start only when euvolaemic. Never in acute decompensation.
C
CRT — Cardiac resynchronisation therapy if LBBB + EF <35% + symptomatic on optimal therapy.
D
Diuretic (furosemide) — Symptom control of fluid overload only.
D
Device (ICD) — If EF <35% + high SCD risk. Or Digoxin for rate control in AF with HF.
Key point: ACE inhibitor and beta-blocker reduce mortality. Diuretics only reduce symptoms.
4
Slide 4 — Admit vs Outpatient + Monitoring
Admit if:
SpO2 <94%, new-onset HF, systolic BP <90, AKI, refractory to oral diuretics, or arrhythmia.
Monitor:
Daily weights — >2kg gain in 2 days = early decompensation. UECs (diuretics + ACEi = watch renal function). BNP trend.
Discharge red flag:
BNP >400 on discharge predicts 30-day readmission. Ensure medications reconciled and follow-up booked.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Never start a beta-blocker in acute decompensated HF — wait until the patient is dry and stable. The commonest reason HF patients re-admit is stopping their medications. Check adherence before escalating therapy. (CTA)

Severe Malaria — When the Picture Changes
Scenario / Twist
1
Slide 1 — Scenario + Original Plan
Scenario
32-year-old man from Kisumu. 4-day history of fever, rigors, and vomiting. RDT positive for P. falciparum. GCS 15, able to take orals, parasite density <2%.
Original Plan
Uncomplicated falciparum malaria. Artemether-lumefantrine (AL) — 4 tablets at 0, 8, 24, 36, 48, and 60 hours. Ensure food before each dose (fat improves absorption). Paracetamol for fever.
2
Slide 2 — Twist
At 12 hours he deteriorates. GCS drops to 10. He vomits the AL dose. Repeat blood smear shows parasite density now 8%. Creatinine has risen to 320μmol/L.
3
Slide 3 — Revised Plan
This is now SEVERE malaria. Switch to IV artesunate 2.4mg/kg at 0, 12, and 24 hours — then once daily. IV fluids cautiously — severe malaria carries risk of pulmonary oedema. Do not fluid overload. Monitor blood glucose every 4 hours — hypoglycaemia is common. Daily FBC — haemolytic anaemia can drop Hb precipitously. Daily UECs — AKI may require dialysis. Hourly GCS — if not improving at 24 hours, rule out bacterial co-infection with LP.
4
Slide 4 — Reasoning
Severe malaria criteria — ANY ONE qualifies: GCS <15, parasite density >5%, AKI, pulmonary oedema, severe anaemia (Hb <7), hypoglycaemia, hyperparasitaemia >10%, acidosis, haemoglobinuria. IV artesunate reduces mortality by 35% compared to quinine in severe disease. Quinine is second-line only if artesunate is unavailable — it causes hypoglycaemia and is arrhythmogenic.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
In Kenya, check glucose at presentation AND every 4 hours in severe malaria — hypoglycaemia kills faster than the parasite. If IV artesunate is unavailable, give rectal artesunate as a bridge to transfer. Never give AL to a vomiting patient — it will not be absorbed. (CTA)

Mnemonics · Know Your Drugs · 1 day
TB Treatment — The RIPE Mnemonic
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
Your patient has confirmed pulmonary TB. GeneXpert positive, rifampicin sensitive, HIV negative. He asks: "How long will I take these drugs and what will happen to my body?" Can you answer him clearly?
2
Slide 2 — RIPE: The Four First-Line Drugs
R
Rifampicin — Bactericidal. Turns body fluids orange — warn the patient. Induces liver enzymes (reduces OCP, warfarin efficacy). 6 months total.
I
Isoniazid — Bactericidal. Give with pyridoxine (Vit B6) to prevent peripheral neuropathy. Hepatotoxic — monitor LFTs. 6 months total.
P
Pyrazinamide — Bactericidal inside macrophages (acidic environment). Causes hyperuricaemia. First 2 months only.
E
Ethambutol — Bacteriostatic. Causes optic neuritis — check visual acuity at baseline. First 2 months only.
3
Slide 3 — Treatment Phases
Intensive phase — 2 months
RIPE — all four drugs. Kills rapidly dividing bacilli.
Continuation phase — 4 months
RI only — rifampicin and isoniazid. Eliminates persistent bacilli.
Extended to 9 months for:
TB meningitis, TB pericarditis, disseminated TB, spinal TB (Pott's disease).
Key point: Completion matters more than speed. Partial treatment = drug resistance.
4
Slide 4 — Monitoring and Pitfalls
Baseline and monitoring
LFTs at baseline. Visual acuity before ethambutol. Recheck LFTs at 2 weeks if abnormal.
Drug interactions
Rifampicin reduces efficacy of OCP, warfarin, antiretrovirals, and fluconazole.
HIV co-infection
Start ART 2–8 weeks after TB treatment initiation. Timing depends on CD4 count.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Always test for HIV at TB diagnosis in Kenya — co-infection rates are high and change management. Defaulting at month 2 is the most dangerous point — the intensive phase is done and only the resistant bacilli remain. Rifampicin turns urine, tears, and sweat orange — counsel the patient before they present in a panic. (CTA)

Mnemonics · Know Your Management · 1 day
Sepsis 6 — The Bundle That Saves Lives
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
45-year-old woman, suspected urinary source. HR 118, RR 22, Temp 38.9°C, BP 94/60, GCS 14. She meets SIRS criteria with signs of organ dysfunction. You have one hour. What do you do?
2
Slide 2 — GIVE 3 (First Hour)
1. Give oxygen
Target SpO2 >94%. High-flow if needed.
2. Give IV fluids
500ml crystalloid bolus over 15–30 minutes. Repeat up to 30ml/kg if hypotensive and not fluid overloaded.
3. Give IV antibiotics
Broad-spectrum within 1 hour of recognition. Every hour of delay increases mortality by 7%. Do not wait for cultures.
Key point: Antibiotics within 1 hour is the single highest-impact intervention in sepsis.
3
Slide 3 — TAKE 3 (Simultaneously)
1. Take blood cultures
x2 sets from different sites before antibiotics if possible. Do not delay antibiotics more than 45 minutes for cultures.
2. Take serum lactate
Lactate >2mmol/L = tissue hypoperfusion. Lactate >4 = septic shock regardless of BP. Repeat at 2 hours.
3. Take urine output
Catheterise and measure hourly. Target >0.5ml/kg/hour. Oliguria = organ dysfunction.
Key point: Lactate is the most important single prognostic marker. Check it immediately.
4
Slide 4 — SIRS vs Sepsis vs Septic Shock
SIRS
2+ of: Temp >38 or <36°C, HR >90, RR >20, WBC >12 or <4.
Sepsis
SIRS + suspected infection + organ dysfunction (SOFA score ≥2).
Septic Shock
Sepsis + vasopressors needed to maintain MAP ≥65 despite adequate fluids + lactate >2.
Key point: Do NOT say "sepsis" without organ dysfunction. Say SIRS secondary to [source].
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Start the Sepsis 6 simultaneously, not sequentially. Noradrenaline is first-line vasopressor — start at 0.01–0.03 mcg/kg/min, titrate to MAP ≥65. Hydrocortisone 200mg/day only if vasopressors are not maintaining MAP despite adequate fluids. (CTA)

Bacterial Meningitis — When the Picture Changes
Scenario / Twist
1
Slide 1 — Scenario + Original Plan
Scenario
19-year-old university student. 2-day history of severe headache, photophobia, neck stiffness, fever 39.2°C. GCS 14. CT head normal. LP: cloudy CSF, WBC 1200 (neutrophilic), elevated protein, low glucose.
Original Plan
Bacterial meningitis. Ceftriaxone 2g IV 12-hourly + dexamethasone 0.15mg/kg IV 6-hourly for 4 days. Supportive care — analgesia, IV fluids, strict input/output monitoring.
2
Slide 2 — Twist
LP also shows India ink positive. CD4 returns at 45 cells/μL. The patient mentions he tested HIV positive 6 months ago but never started ART.
3
Slide 3 — Revised Plan
Revised diagnosis: Cryptococcal meningitis in HIV. Stop ceftriaxone. Induction (2 weeks): Amphotericin B 0.7–1mg/kg/day IV + Flucytosine 25mg/kg QID. Consolidation (8 weeks): Fluconazole 400mg/day orally. Maintenance: Fluconazole 200mg/day lifelong or until immune reconstitution. ICP management: Therapeutic LP daily if opening pressure >25cmH2O. Remove 20–30ml CSF. Do not give mannitol or acetazolamide. ART: Start 4–6 weeks after antifungal initiation — not immediately (risk of IRIS).
4
Slide 4 — Reasoning
Cryptococcal meningitis is the leading cause of adult meningitis in HIV-positive patients in sub-Saharan Africa. India ink confirms Cryptococcus neoformans. High ICP from fungal polysaccharide capsule is the main cause of mortality — therapeutic LP is the most important intervention. Starting ART too early causes Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS), which worsens meningitis.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
In Kenya, always check HIV status in any patient with meningitis. Subacute onset + HIV = Cryptococcus first. Fluconazole alone as induction is inferior — do not shortcut the amphotericin phase. Headache worsening on treatment means check the opening pressure, not the antifungal choice. (CTA)

Eclampsia — MgSO4 Protocol and CALL Monitoring
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
22-year-old primigravida at 36 weeks. Actively seizing. Last recorded BP 170/115. Second seizure in 30 minutes. You are the only doctor available. What do you do in the next 5 minutes?
2
Slide 2 — MgSO4 Pritchard Protocol
Loading dose
MgSO4 4g IV over 15–20 minutes (dilute in 100ml Normal Saline). Do NOT give as a bolus.
Maintenance
MgSO4 1g/hour IV infusion for 24 hours after the last seizure.
Recurrent seizure on MgSO4
Additional 2g IV bolus over 5 minutes.
No IV access
10g IM — 5g each buttock, deeply. Loading dose only.
Key point: MgSO4 is not a sedative. It prevents eclamptic seizures via NMDA receptor blockade.
3
Slide 3 — Monitor for Toxicity: CALL
C
Check patellar reflex — Loss = first sign of toxicity (~5mmol/L). Stop infusion immediately.
A
Assess RR — <12/min = stop infusion immediately.
L
Level of consciousness — Confusion or drowsiness = toxicity.
L
Low urine output — Mg is renally cleared. If UO <25ml/hour, halve the infusion rate.
Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1g IV over 10 minutes. Keep at the bedside at all times.
4
Slide 4 — Control BP and Deliver
Target BP
Systolic 140–150, Diastolic 90–100. Do NOT drop BP too fast.
First-line acute
Hydralazine 5mg IV bolus every 15–20 minutes (max 20mg). Or labetalol 20mg IV. Oral: nifedipine 10mg.
Avoid
ACE inhibitors (contraindicated in pregnancy). Nitroprusside (fetal cyanide toxicity).
Definitive treatment
Delivery. Eclampsia is only cured by delivery.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
MgSO4 reduces eclampsia recurrence by 52%. Never give diazepam as first-line — it sedates but does not prevent recurrence. Deliver within 12 hours of stabilisation regardless of gestational age. Postpartum eclampsia can occur up to 6 weeks after delivery — always check BP at postnatal visits. (CTA)

Mnemonics · Know Your Management · 1 day
DKA — A Systematic Approach
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
20-year-old type 1 diabetic. Drowsy (GCS 12). Deep rapid breathing (Kussmaul respiration). Fruity breath. Blood glucose 28mmol/L. Bicarbonate 8mmol/L. pH 7.12. She is in severe DKA. You have the first hour to get it right.
2
Slide 2 — DKA Criteria and Severity
All three must be present
Hyperglycaemia: BG >11mmol/L. Acidosis: pH <7.3 OR bicarb <15mmol/L. Ketonaemia: blood ketones >3mmol/L OR urine ketones 2+.
Severity grading
Mild: pH 7.25–7.30 · Moderate: pH 7.00–7.24 · Severe: pH <7.00.
Key point: Euglycaemic DKA exists — especially on SGLT2 inhibitors. Check ketones regardless of glucose level.
3
Slide 3 — Fluids First
0.9% Normal Saline
1L over 1 hour → 1L over 2 hours → 1L over 2 hours → 1L over 4 hours. Adjust based on clinical state and urine output.
Switch when glucose <14mmol/L
0.45% NaCl with 5% dextrose — allows continued insulin infusion without causing hypoglycaemia.
Potassium
Add KCl to fluids once K <5.5mmol/L and urine output confirmed. DKA causes total body K depletion even if serum K appears normal initially.
4
Slide 4 — Insulin and Resolution
Fixed rate IV insulin
0.1 units/kg/hour. Do NOT give a bolus. Do NOT stop insulin until ketones cleared — even if glucose is normal, switch fluid to dextrose/saline instead.
Monitor
Hourly glucose and ketones. 2-hourly UECs and VBG. Strict fluid balance.
Resolution criteria
pH >7.3, bicarb >18, ketones <0.6, tolerating orals. Give long-acting insulin SC, then stop infusion 1 hour later.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
The commonest DKA death in young patients is cerebral oedema from over-rapid fluid correction. Do not give hypotonic fluids. The second commonest is hypokalaemia — insulin drives K intracellularly. Check K every 2 hours and replace aggressively. Bicarbonate is not routinely given — only consider if pH <6.9. (CTA)

Sickle Cell Crisis — When Pain Progresses to Something Worse
Scenario / Twist
1
Slide 1 — Scenario + Original Plan
Scenario
16-year-old with known HbSS. Severe bilateral leg and back pain 9/10. Temp 37.4°C, SpO2 97%, HR 108. Fourth admission this year. No fever, normal CXR, normal urine.
Original Plan
Vaso-occlusive pain crisis. Ibuprofen 400mg TDS + morphine 0.1mg/kg IV PRN 2–4 hourly. IV fluids 1.5x maintenance. Oxygen only if SpO2 <95%. Incentive spirometry. Monitor for acute chest syndrome.
2
Slide 2 — Twist
At 18 hours, despite adequate morphine: fever 38.8°C, new bilateral infiltrates on CXR, SpO2 drops to 90% on 4L O2, and Hb drops from 7.2 to 5.8g/dL.
3
Slide 3 — Revised Plan
New diagnosis: Acute Chest Syndrome (ACS) — the leading cause of death in sickle cell disease. Increase O2. Consider NIV or intubation if SpO2 not responding. Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg daily (cover atypicals). Exchange transfusion: Urgent — reduces HbS% to <30%. Simple transfusion if exchange unavailable. Bronchodilators: Salbutamol nebulisation (bronchospasm component common). Incentive spirometry every 2 hours. Admit to ICU/HDU — ACS can progress to respiratory failure within hours.
4
Slide 4 — Reasoning
ACS diagnosis: New pulmonary infiltrate on CXR + one or more of fever, chest pain, cough, hypoxia, tachypnoea. Aetiology: fat embolism from infarcted bone marrow, infection (S. pneumoniae, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia), and in situ pulmonary sickling. Exchange transfusion is superior to simple transfusion — reduces viscosity and HbS percentage simultaneously.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Every sickle cell admission needs a baseline SpO2 and CXR — ACS can develop in a patient who arrives without respiratory symptoms. Hydroxyurea reduces crisis frequency by 50% — if your patient has more than 3 crises per year, they need it. Penicillin prophylaxis and pneumococcal vaccination reduce ACS risk. (CTA)

Anaemia in Pregnancy — WHO Thresholds and FIND IT
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
24-year-old G2P1 at 28 weeks. Fatigue, dizziness, exertional dyspnoea. Hb 8.6g/dL on routine iron and folic acid supplementation. How do you classify her anaemia and what do you do next?
2
Slide 2 — WHO Thresholds in Pregnancy
Normal in pregnancy
Hb ≥11.0g/dL. Physiological haemodilution is expected.
Mild anaemia
Hb 10.0–10.9g/dL.
Moderate anaemia
Hb 7.0–9.9g/dL. This patient: Hb 8.6 = moderate.
Severe anaemia
Hb <7.0g/dL.
Key point: Hb 10.5 in a pregnant woman IS anaemia. Hb 10.5 in a non-pregnant woman is NOT. Know your thresholds.
3
Slide 3 — FIND IT: Why Is She Anaemic?
F
Folate deficiency — Inadequate supplementation, multiple pregnancy, haemolysis.
I
Iron deficiency — Most common in Kenya. Poor dietary intake, malabsorption.
N
Natural haemolysis — Malaria. Always screen with RDT/thick film in endemic areas.
D
Deficiency of B12 — Rare but consider in strict vegetarians.
I
Infection — Hookworm, HIV, chronic infection.
T
Thalassaemia/Sickle cell — Haemoglobinopathy screen if Hb not responding to treatment.
4
Slide 4 — Management by Severity
Mild (Hb 10–10.9)
Oral ferrous sulfate 200mg TDS + folic acid 5mg daily. Review at 4 weeks.
Moderate (Hb 7–9.9)
Oral iron. Consider IV iron sucrose if non-responsive or malabsorption suspected. Treat underlying cause.
Severe (Hb <7)
IV iron or blood transfusion based on symptoms. If cardiac compromise or severe dyspnoea — transfuse. Admit.
Always treat malaria first if co-infection present — Hb will not recover without treating the underlying cause.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
In Kenya, always pair anaemia workup with malaria screening and deworming (mebendazole after first trimester). If Hb does not rise after 4 weeks of adequate oral iron, something is being missed — check blood film, reticulocyte count, and haemoglobin electrophoresis. Severe anaemia at delivery is a leading contributor to maternal mortality. (CTA)

Pneumonia Severity — CURB-65
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
58-year-old man, 4-day productive cough, fever, right-sided pleuritic pain. CXR confirms right lower lobe consolidation. BP 88/60, urea 12mmol/L, RR 28, mildly confused. How do you risk-stratify him and decide where to manage him?
2
Slide 2 — CURB-65: One Point Each
C
Confusion — New onset, AMT ≤8.
U
Urea — >7mmol/L.
R
Respiratory rate — ≥30 breaths/minute.
B
Blood pressure — Systolic <90 or diastolic ≤60.
65
Age — ≥65 years.
This patient: C✓ U✓ R✓ B✓ = Score 4. 30-day mortality ~27%. Score 0–1: home. Score 2: consider admission. Score 3–5: hospital/ICU.
3
Slide 3 — Antibiotic Selection: Kenya Context
Mild (outpatient)
Amoxicillin 1g TDS x5–7 days. Add azithromycin if atypical organism suspected.
Moderate (inpatient)
IV ceftriaxone 2g daily + azithromycin 500mg daily.
Severe (ICU)
IV ceftriaxone + IV azithromycin. Escalate to piperacillin-tazobactam if hospital-acquired or aspiration suspected.
Always exclude TB: Any consolidation not responding at 48–72 hours = sputum AFB. Do not assume CAP.
4
Slide 4 — When to Escalate
Not improving at 48–72 hours
Consider: wrong diagnosis (TB, PE, malignancy), resistant organism, empyema (USS chest), wrong dose or route.
Pleural effusion
Aspirate if >50% hemithorax, loculated, or patient not improving. Send for LDH, protein, glucose, pH, MC&S, ZN stain.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
CURB-65 underestimates severity in young patients with no comorbidities — use clinical judgement alongside the score. In Kenya, screen for HIV in any CAP admission. Upper lobe consolidation + young patient + cough >2 weeks = TB first, CAP second. Blood cultures before antibiotics in all admitted patients. (CTA)

Typhoid Fever — When First-Line Treatment Fails
Scenario / Twist
1
Slide 1 — Scenario + Original Plan
Scenario
17-year-old student from Nairobi. 10-day stepwise rising fever, headache, relative bradycardia, rose spots, constipation. Widal: O antigen 1:160. Blood culture: Salmonella typhi.
Original Plan
Typhoid fever confirmed. Ciprofloxacin 500mg BD orally for 10–14 days. Paracetamol, IV fluids, strict isolation. Monitor for complications.
2
Slide 2 — Twist
Sensitivity returns showing resistance to ciprofloxacin, ampicillin, and cotrimoxazole — a pattern consistent with extensively drug-resistant (XDR) typhoid.
3
Slide 3 — Revised Plan
Switch to: Azithromycin 1g daily orally x7 days — first-line for uncomplicated XDR typhoid. Severe XDR typhoid (GCS depression, perforation, shock): Meropenem 1g IV TDS x10–14 days. Source control: Cultures from close contacts. Report to public health — XDR typhoid is notifiable. Monitor for complications: Intestinal perforation (sudden peritonism = surgical emergency), haemorrhage, myocarditis, hepatitis.
4
Slide 4 — Reasoning
XDR Salmonella typhi carries resistance genes on a transferable plasmid — first emerged in Pakistan 2016, now reported across East Africa. Azithromycin achieves high intracellular concentration and is effective for uncomplicated disease. Carbapenems reserved for severe disease. Ceftriaxone resistance is emerging — do not assume it works without sensitivity data.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Do not treat typhoid empirically with ciprofloxacin in Kenya without sensitivity data — resistance rates are rising. The Widal test has low specificity in endemic areas — blood culture is the gold standard. Week 3 is when perforation peaks — if your typhoid patient suddenly develops acute abdomen, this is a surgical emergency with >30% mortality. (CTA)

Mnemonics · Know Your Labs · 1 day
High Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis — MUDPILES
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
ABG returns: pH 7.18, pCO2 28, HCO3 10, Na 138, Cl 102. Anion gap = 138 – (102+10) = 26 (normal 8–12). You have a high anion gap metabolic acidosis. The ABG tells you the what. MUDPILES tells you the why.
2
Slide 2 — MUDPILES
M
Methanol — Illicit alcohol ingestion. Visual symptoms. Treat with ethanol/fomepizole + dialysis.
U
Uraemia — Renal failure, organic acid accumulation. Check UECs and creatinine.
D
DKA — Insulin deficiency, ketoacidosis. Check ketones and glucose.
P
Propylene glycol — IV medication solvent (lorazepam, phenobarbitone infusions).
I
Isoniazid / Iron toxicity — TB medication history or ingestion.
L
Lactic acidosis — Most common. Sepsis, shock, metformin in AKI, liver failure.
E
Ethylene glycol — Antifreeze ingestion. Calcium oxalate crystaluria. Fomepizole + dialysis.
S
Salicylates — Aspirin overdose. Mixed respiratory alkalosis + metabolic acidosis pattern.
3
Slide 3 — ABG Interpretation Step by Step
Step 1
pH — acidosis (<7.35) or alkalosis (>7.45)?
Step 2
Primary disturbance — pCO2 (respiratory) or HCO3 (metabolic)?
Step 3
Appropriate compensation? Winter's formula for metabolic acidosis: expected pCO2 = (1.5 × HCO3) + 8 ±2.
Step 4
Anion gap = Na – (Cl + HCO3). Adjust for albumin: add 2.5 per 10g/L albumin below 40.
Step 5
AG elevated = MUDPILES. AG normal = HARDUPS (hyperchloraemic acidosis).
4
Slide 4 — Kenya Context: Most Common Causes
Lactic acidosis
Sepsis (most common), severe malaria (impaired hepatic lactate clearance), metformin in AKI.
DKA
Type 1 DM, new-onset type 2, omitted insulin.
Uraemia
Advanced CKD, AKI from any cause.
Key point: Check lactate in any unwell patient with metabolic acidosis — it is the quickest answer.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
A normal-appearing bicarbonate can mask severe acidosis if concurrent metabolic alkalosis exists (vomiting + DKA). Calculate delta-delta if AG is elevated: (AG–12)/(24–HCO3). Result >2 = concurrent metabolic alkalosis. Result <1 = concurrent normal AG metabolic acidosis. The ABG never lies — the interpretation does. (CTA)

Preeclampsia — ABCDE Management
Mnemonic
1
Slide 1 — Scenario
19-year-old primigravida at 34 weeks. Headache, visual disturbances, epigastric pain. BP 158/104 on two readings 4 hours apart. Urine PCR 45mg/mmol. Platelets 92. LFTs elevated. What are you dealing with and what do you do?
2
Slide 2 — Diagnosis and Severity
Preeclampsia definition
BP ≥140/90 x2, ≥4 hours apart after 20 weeks + proteinuria (PCR ≥30mg/mmol or dipstick ≥2+) OR end-organ dysfunction even without proteinuria.
Severe features (ANY ONE)
BP ≥160/110, platelets <100, creatinine >97, LFTs >2× normal, pulmonary oedema, new headache/visual changes, epigastric pain.
This patient: BP + proteinuria + low platelets + elevated LFTs + symptoms = Severe Preeclampsia with HELLP.
3
Slide 3 — Management: ABCDE
A
Antihypertensives — Target BP <150/100. IV labetalol or hydralazine for acute severe BP. Oral nifedipine for maintenance.
B
Blood tests — FBC, UECs, LFTs, LDH, coagulation, uric acid — at diagnosis and 6–12 hourly if deteriorating.
C
Control seizures — MgSO4 prophylaxis for severe features. Pritchard protocol.
D
Deliver — ≥37 weeks = deliver now. 34–37 weeks with severe features = deliver after steroids. <34 weeks = multidisciplinary decision.
E
Evaluate fetus — CTG, biophysical profile, Dopplers. Betamethasone 12mg IM x2 doses 24 hours apart if <34 weeks.
4
Slide 4 — What NOT to Do
Do NOT give furosemide routinely
Preeclampsia patients are intravascularly volume-depleted despite oedema. Diuretics worsen placental perfusion.
Do NOT delay MgSO4
Give before transfer if referring. Do not withhold it waiting for "one more reading."
Do NOT discharge without follow-up
BP can spike in the first 48 hours postpartum. Postpartum preeclampsia can develop up to 6 weeks after delivery.
5
Slide 5 — Clinical Pearl
Preeclampsia is a disease of the placenta — delivery is the cure, not the last resort. Antihypertensives and MgSO4 are bridges to delivery, not alternatives to it. Hypertensive disease of pregnancy is the second leading cause of maternal death in Kenya. Know this condition well. (CTA)
